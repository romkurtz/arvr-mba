Slide 2 / Context

Why the US Market Matters

The US is the biggest and fastest growing healthcare VR / AR market in the world.

$1.14B
US healthcare AR / VR market size in 2025[1]
$2.34B
Forecast for 2030 — 15.4% CAGR (yearly growth rate)[1]
~10%
US RN (Registered Nurse) shortage in 2026 — ~350K missing FTEs (Full Time Equivalents)[2]

Big pain point: training capacity

Nursing schools turned down 80,000+ applicants because they did not have enough faculty, classrooms or clinical sites. VR lets schools and hospitals train more nurses, faster, cheaper.[3]

Investors and hospitals are buying

Healthcare is the fastest growing VR vertical in the US — ~27% yearly growth. The FDA has cleared 69 therapeutic VR devices (as of Sep 2024) and CMS now reimburses some VR codes.[4]

Sources: [1] Grand View Research, [2] Prolink / HRSA, [3] Gaumard / AACN, [4] Mordor Intelligence. Full links on the References slide.

Slide 3 / Glossary

Short Glossary — All The Initials

Quick reference for every short word we use in this deck.

AR
Augmented Reality — digital things added on top of the real world (like glasses showing the patient's veins on the skin).
VR
Virtual Reality — you put a headset and you are inside a fully digital world (like a virtual hospital room).
XR
Extended Reality — the umbrella word for AR + VR + Mixed Reality.
HMD
Head Mounted Display — the headset you wear (Meta Quest, Apple Vision Pro, HoloLens).
FDA
Food and Drug Administration — US authority that approves medical devices and software.
510(k)
FDA clearance path for medical devices that are similar to ones already on the market.
CMS
Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services — the US government healthcare payer.
CPT
Current Procedural Terminology — the billing codes hospitals use to get paid.
RN / LPN
Registered Nurse / Licensed Practical Nurse — the two main nurse roles in the US.
EMS
Emergency Medical Services — paramedics, ambulance crews.
DOD / DAF
Department of Defense / Department of the Air Force — US military buyers.
SBIR / STTR
Small Business Innovation Research / Tech Transfer — US government grants for startups.
CAGR
Compound Annual Growth Rate — the average yearly growth percentage.
GTM
Go To Market — the strategy for how a company sells and reaches customers.
B2B
Business to Business — selling to companies / hospitals (not consumers).
SaaS
Software as a Service — software sold by yearly subscription.
DICOM
Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine — the standard file format for CT / MRI scans.
CT / MRI
Computed Tomography / Magnetic Resonance Imaging — medical imaging machines.
CMF / T
Craniomaxillofacial / Trauma — surgery of the face, skull and trauma cases.
TCCC
Tactical Combat Casualty Care — military battlefield medical protocol.
ACLS
Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support — emergency cardiac protocol that all clinicians must certify on.
OB / OBGYN
Obstetrics / Obstetrics-Gynecology — childbirth and women's health.
FTE
Full Time Equivalent — one full time job worth of hours.
HRSA
Health Resources and Services Administration — US federal health workforce agency.
Slide 4 / Map

The 3 Competitive Layers in US Healthcare XR

ARVR Israel competes mostly in Layer 1 (nursing training). Layers 2 and 3 are adjacent — we should know them so we can position smart.

Layer 1: Direct competitors

VR training for nurses, EMS and basic procedures. Same buyer (nursing schools, hospital training departments), same problem.

UbiSim, Oxford Medical Simulation (OMS), Health Scholars / Relias, SimX.

Layer 2: Adjacent competitors

VR surgical training. Different buyer (surgeons, medical device companies) but same "VR in healthcare" budget pool.

Osso VR, FundamentalVR, PrecisionOS.

Layer 3: Related (AR in the OR)

AR for real surgeries — planning and navigation, not training. Useful to understand the full XR landscape.

Medivis, ImmersiveTouch, Augmedics.

Why this matters for ARVR Israel: the most competitive zone is nursing / procedural training (Layer 1). The good news — only 3 to 4 serious players, and the market is much bigger than any of them today. Plenty of room. The bad news — UbiSim (owned by Labster) and Osso VR (entering nursing) are well funded and moving fast.
Slide 5 / Layer 1 — Direct Competitor
UbiSim (by Labster)
Direct competitor VR nursing training Boston, USA
Slide 5

The most direct competitor — "the world's first VR training platform made by nurses for nurses". Almost the same pitch as ARVR Israel.[5]

Focus
VR clinical scenarios for nursing students — clinical judgment, communication, patient-centric care.[6]
Funding / Ownership
Acquired by Labster in Oct 2021[5]. Labster total funding: $147M, last round $47M in 2022.[7]
Reach
Labster + UbiSim reach 6 million learners in 3,000 institutions across 100 countries (Jan 2025).[8]
Awards / Validation
Named TIME Best Invention of 2024.[8] AI Narrative Analysis launched Dec 2025.[9]
Hardware & Partnerships
Runs on Meta Quest VR headsets. Three-year partnership with HCT — the Philippines' largest nursing curriculum provider, supported by Meta.[8]
Pricing model
B2B SaaS subscription to nursing schools and hospital systems — "Intuitive Editor" so faculty can build their own scenarios.[6]
Threat level: HIGH. Almost identical positioning to ARVR. Already at scale in the US with strong parent company (Labster, backed by Andreessen Horowitz). ARVR needs a clear differentiation — deeper procedural realism, specific verticals like geriatrics or rehab (where ARVR already wins in Israel), or better price for community colleges.

Slide 12 / Layer 3 — Related (AR in the OR)

AR in the Operating Room — Different Product, Same Buyer Budget

These companies don't train clinicians — they help surgeons see better during real operations. Listed so we understand the broader XR (Extended Reality) landscape competing for hospital innovation dollars.

Medivis — Brooklyn, NY

AR surgical navigation for the operating room (OR). World's first FDA-cleared AR system for cranial neurosurgery (Dec 2025) — eligible for CMS reimbursement codes CPT 61781 / 61783.[34]

Earlier SurgicalAR platform was FDA 510(k) cleared in 2019. Runs on Microsoft HoloLens AR glasses.[35]

ImmersiveTouch — Chicago, IL

VR + AR for patient-specific surgical planning, models, and custom 3D-printed guides. Used in 3,000+ cases at 100+ healthcare facilities.[36]

Acquired by HealthpointCapital (PE) in June 2025 — deploying from a $200M musculoskeletal-focused fund.[37]

Threat level: LOW (today). These tools sell to surgeons and OR managers — not nursing schools. But they compete for the same hospital "innovation / XR" line item. They also create awareness and trust for medical XR with the FDA — this is good news for ARVR.

Slide 14 / Takeaways

5 Takeaways for ARVR Israel

What this competitive landscape means for our US market entry strategy.

1
The nursing-only lane has only 2 real US-active players — UbiSim and OMS (Oxford Medical Simulation). The market is much bigger than what they cover today. There is room for a 3rd player — but only with clear differentiation.
2
Distribution wins, not technology. Health Scholars (via Relias) and Osso VR (via America's Essential Hospitals) bought reach with one deal. ARVR should hunt for a US distribution partner — a hospital network, GPO (Group Purchasing Organization), or LMS — before hiring sales reps.
3
Government money is real money. SimX took $20M+ from the US Air Force. The SBIR / STTR program is open to small Israeli-founded US companies. We should evaluate this path early.
4
"Made by nurses for nurses" is already taken by UbiSim. ARVR's stronger angle: "Tested in real Israeli hospital chaos — MDA, rehab, geriatrics", which competitors do not cover. We should pick 1-2 underserved verticals (e.g., rehabilitation, geriatrics, mass-casualty / EMS) and lead with them.
5
Apple Vision Pro is the next platform race. FundamentalVR is already on it. Being early on Vision Pro could be a cheap way to get on the radar of Mayo Clinic, Stanford and the trade press — especially with US-style cinematic content.
Slide 15 / Bonus Market — UAE

UAE — Maybe Our Smarter First Move Abroad

The US is the biggest prize, no question. But for a 3-person team in Tel Aviv with limited cash and time, the UAE (United Arab Emirates) may actually be the smarter first international market — smaller in absolute size, but much easier to reach, less crowded, and politically wide open today thanks to the Abraham Accords.

$2.5B
Middle East AR / VR healthcare market size in 2025 — the UAE is the leading country in the region[39]
$10.2B
Forecast for 2035 — 15.5% CAGR (yearly growth rate) for the regional AR / VR healthcare market[39]
20%
CAGR of VR for medical training — the fastest growing sub-segment in the region. This is exactly ARVR's lane[39]

Who buys VR training in the UAE?

  • Dubai Health + MBRU (Mohammed Bin Rashid University) — already piloting VR for pain reduction at the Thalassemia Center.[40]
  • MoHAP (Ministry of Health and Prevention) — launched a 3D / VR project to make nursing more attractive to students.[41]
  • SEHA / PureHealth — UAE's largest network, 14 hospitals + 70+ clinics + 3,000+ beds. Centralized buyer.[42]
  • Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi — runs the UAE's largest simulation center (24,000 sq ft) — already uses VR equipment.[43]
  • Khalifa University KU-CELS — first UAE simulation center with US dual accreditation (Society for Simulation in Healthcare). Has a CAVE (Cave Automated Virtual Environment) immersive room.[44]

The Israeli angle — why now?

Since the 2020 Abraham Accords, Israeli health institutions sell openly into the UAE.[45]

  • Sheba Medical Center × G42 Healthcare (Abu Dhabi) — strategic MOU (Memorandum Of Understanding) on AI / clinical research / training.[46]
  • Sheba × Abu Dhabi Department of Health — signed MOU for healthcare service development.[45]

An Israeli VR training startup can ride on this open door — but should be ready for political volatility.

Our recommendation: For ARVR Israel as a small founder-led team, the UAE is realistically the smarter first stop abroad — not because it is bigger, but because it is reachable. Only ~5-6 buyers control most of the public health budget (vs. thousands of independent buyers in the US). Local competition is just 4 small project shops (vs. 8+ well-funded US scale-ups burning $100M+ each). Tel Aviv to Dubai is a 3-hour flight; Tel Aviv to New York is 12. And the Abraham Accords door is wide open today — tomorrow it may not be. Winning Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi, SEHA or a Sheba × G42-style joint program first gives ARVR a flagship reference customer that then opens the much harder US doors. The US stays the long-term prize. The UAE is the smarter on-ramp.

Sources: [39] Emergen Research, [40] MBRU, [41] MoHAP, [42] Morningstar / Lunit-SEHA, [43] Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi, [44] Khalifa University, [45] Abraham Accords Peace Institute, [46] Sheba Global.

Slide 16 / UAE Competitors

Who Already Sells VR Healthcare in the UAE?

The competitive bench is much shorter than the US — a mix of small local XR (Extended Reality) shops and a few global players that landed inside top hospitals.

PROVEN Reality — SimBios Direct

UAE / KSA-based AR / VR studio. "SimBios" is a VR healthcare simulation platform covering ultrasound, auscultation (listening to heart / lungs), CT (Computed Tomography) and patient exams. Deployed via SideQuest on Meta Quest. Markets itself as "the leading AR / VR provider in UAE and Saudi Arabia".[47]

SingulXR — Dubai Direct

Dubai-based (JLT) custom VR / AR / AI shop. Offers "healthcare simulation training" and "AI + XR medical simulations". Claims deployments in 12 countries. Project-based model rather than off-the-shelf product.[48]

Wallfort.tech Direct

AR / VR hospital staff training with simulations tailored to UAE realities — heatstroke, mass casualty events, Cleveland Clinic / Thumbay environments, multicultural patient communication. Strong local positioning, smaller player.[49]

XRDigitech — Dubai Adjacent

Dubai IT / XR services company with a "Metaverse Medical Simulator" — patient simulations, 3D anatomy and emergency response training. Sells to educators and corporates, not just hospitals.[50]

Global players already inside UAE hospitals Foreign

  • Body Interact (Portugal) — virtual patient simulator used inside Khalifa University KU-CELS.[51]
  • CAE Healthcare (US / Canada) — learning management system + manikin simulators used at KU-CELS.[51]
  • Oxford Medical Simulation (OMS), UbiSim and Osso VR all sell globally and could enter UAE through their existing distributor networks. None have a UAE-dedicated offering yet.

What is missing in the UAE today?

  • A clear nursing-focused VR brand — UbiSim's lane is open here.
  • Arabic-language scenarios with culturally adapted patient avatars.
  • Bundled offering for the public sector (SEHA + MoHAP) — ARVR's hospital-tested Israeli scenarios (MDA, geriatrics, rehab) would fit straight in.
  • Strong academic publications in the region — MBRU and Khalifa want research partners, not just vendors.
Threat level in UAE: LOW to MEDIUM. No dominant local player. The 4 UAE startups are mostly small project shops, not scaled products. The bigger risk is a US / UK player (UbiSim, OMS, Osso VR) landing a flagship deal with SEHA or Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi before ARVR does. The "first credible Israel-UAE healthcare VR partnership" is still up for grabs — that is the wedge.

Sources: [47] PROVEN Reality, [48] SingulXR, [49] Wallfort.tech, [50] XRDigitech, [51] Khalifa University KU-CELS.
